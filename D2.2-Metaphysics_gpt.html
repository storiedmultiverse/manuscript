This section is mainly based on a philosophy textbook by Loux (2006) and occasionally references other sources, aiming to show various well-regarded theories about what truly exists.

Metaphysical Realism

The idea here is simple: there are real things like dogs and cats, and these things exist regardless of what we call them or want them to be. Plato believed that a dog, as a species, exists as a real and unchanging form in a metaphysical world of formsâ€”more real than any individual dog we see. He illustrated this with a story about people in a dark cave, seeing only shadows, which they think are the only real things.

Metaphysical realists believe everything is either a particular (like shadows) or a universal (like the forms casting the shadows), and both are equally real. Loux describes particulars as unique, temporary entities that don't embody other things but can change and have physical parts.

Universals, like properties, kinds, and relations, don't take up space and are abstract. They include things like numbers and concepts, which exist outside of time and space and don't affect or get affected by the physical world.

Nominalism

Nominalists argue that only specific, individual objects (particulars) exist, and any discussion of universals (like redness or beauty) is just a complicated way of talking about these objects. They question the reality of abstract concepts unless they can somehow be linked to real-world objects.

Propositions and Facts, States of Affairs, Events

We use "propositions" to describe truths about the world, such as "this apple is red," which implies a specific relationship between the apple and the color red. These propositions are abstract and don't exist in the physical world but are considered real by metaphysical realists.

Possible Worlds

The concept of possible worlds helps explain different ways the world could be. It includes not just what is real here and now but also what could potentially exist or happen. This theory expands our understanding of reality, considering not only the actual world but also countless other possible scenarios.

Multiverse Theories in Physics

Physicists' multiverse theories suggest that our universe might be just one of many with different physical laws. These theories range from simple ideas that there are infinite physical universes to complex notions involving multiple dimensions and fundamental laws of physics that differ from our own.
